How the world will miss its universal education targets

The world will be 50 years late meeting its education commitments if current trends continue, with just 11 countries projected to meet the Sustainable Development Goal of ensuring universal secondary education by 2030.

The projections have been released as part of UNESCO’s 2016 Global Education Monitoring report, which details many of the knock on effects that missed education commitments will have for development.

Millennium Development Goal 4.1
Ensure that, by 2015, children everywhere, boys and girls alike, will be able to complete a full course of primary schooling

Which countries achieved universal primary education by 2015?

There is a noticeable difference between high and low income countries when it comes to achieving universal education, and, despite significant investment in primary education since the turn of the millennium, the Millennium Development Goal (MDG) of achieving universal primary education by 2015 has been missed by almost 60% of countries.

Are we on track to meet last year’s Sustainable Development Goals?

In September 2015 the Sustainable Development Goals were agreed at the United Nations Sustainable Development Summit. Among them was a commitment to achieve universal secondary education by 2030.

Sustainable Development Goal 4.1
By 2030, ensure that all girls and boys complete free, equitable and quality primary and secondary education leading to relevant and effective learning outcomes

Current trends indicate that only a handful of countries will achieve this goal, with the majority of the world falling far behind the 2030 target.

When will the remaining countries reach universal education?

Universal primary education

Despite being a target for 2015, eleven countries won’t achieve universal primary education until 2100, including Niger, Rwanda and the Central African Republic, putting them over 100 years behind high-income countries such as Austria, the US and Germany.

Universal secondary education

Over 40% of countries won’t meet the Sustainable Development Goal’s aim of universal secondary education by 2030, including the high income countries of Aruba, Uruguay, Trinidad & Tobago and the Bahamas. Nepal and Zimbabwe are projected to be the first low income countries to meet the target in 2090, 60 years behind the SDG.

What impact will this have?

Reduced infant mortality

Achieving universal secondary completion would make a large contribution towards reducing infant mortality, with an estimated 300,000 - 350,000 fewer child deaths per year by 2050.

Most of these children would be saved in sub-Saharan Africa, as the largest gains in child health comes from primary and lower-secondary education, improvements of which are already included in the trend scenario for most other continents.

Poverty in low income countries could be massively reduced

If universal secondary education could be achieved by 2030 it could bring poverty elimination forward by 10 years.

If SDG 4.1 was met, the poverty headcount rate (the proportion of the population living below the poverty line of US$1.25 at 2005 purchasing power parity) in low income countries would drop to 24% by 2030, compared to 28% at current trends. By 2050, it would be projected to drop to 2%, instead of the 9% currently forecasted.

With 875 million people currently living in low income countries, this would lift 60 million people out of poverty.
